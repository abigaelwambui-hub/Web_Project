The Architecture of Silence

Silence is rarely empty. If you sit still long enough in an old house, you begin to realize that the absence of noise is actually a heavy, physical thing. It presses against the windowpanes alongside the rain, demanding to be acknowledged.

We spend our lives running from it. We fill our rooms with the hum of radios, the artificial glow of screens, the constant chatter of meaningless conversations—all to avoid facing the architecture of our own minds.

But when the power goes out, or when the rest of the world finally falls asleep, the silence remains. It is the ultimate truth-teller. It does not allow you to hide behind excuses or distractions. It forces you to sit at your desk, pick up a pen, and confront the thousands of thoughts you've been avoiding all day.

Perhaps that is why I write. Not to break the silence, but to finally translate what it has been trying to tell me all along.

Letters to an Empty Room

He left the letters on the desk, precisely where they had been written. Twelve envelopes, unsealed, addressed to a ghost who hadn't walked through that door in a decade. The ink was still fresh enough to smudge, a testament to a conversation that would never happen.

Writing them was a ritual. Every year, when the frost first touched the window, he would pull out the heavy parchment paper and uncap the silver fountain pen. The scratching of the nib was the only sound in the study.

Some letters were angry. Others were apologies wrapped in metaphor. But this final letter, the twelfth one, was simply an acknowledgment of time. He wrote about the dust gathering on the bookshelves, the way the floorboards creaked differently now, and how the memory of her was no longer a sharp pain, but a dull, permanent ache.

He set the pen down. For the first time in ten years, he sealed the envelopes. He didn't mail them—there was no address to send them to. Instead, he locked them in the bottom drawer, turned off the brass lamp, and finally walked out of the room.
